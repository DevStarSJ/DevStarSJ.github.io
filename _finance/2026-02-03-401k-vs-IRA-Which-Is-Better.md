# 401(k) vs IRA: Which Retirement Account Is Better for You?

Planning for retirement? You've probably heard of 401(k)s and IRAs, but understanding the differences can be confusing. Let's break down both accounts so you can make the best choice for your future.

## Quick Overview

| Feature | 401(k) | IRA |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Offered by | Employer | You open it yourself |
| 2026 Contribution Limit | $23,500 | $7,000 |
| Employer Match | Yes (if offered) | No |
| Investment Options | Limited to plan options | Almost unlimited |
| Required Minimum Distributions | Yes, at age 73 | Traditional: Yes / Roth: No |

## What Is a 401(k)?

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement plan. Your contributions come directly from your paycheck before taxes (Traditional) or after taxes (Roth 401k).

### Key Features of 401(k)

**Contribution Limits (2026):**
- Under 50: $23,500
- Age 50+: $31,000 (includes $7,500 catch-up)

**Employer Match:**
The biggest advantage! Many employers match your contributions:
- Common match: 50% up to 6% of salary
- Example: You earn $100,000, contribute 6% ($6,000)
- Employer adds $3,000 = **$9,000 total**

This is FREE MONEY. Always contribute enough to get the full match.

**Tax Treatment (Traditional 401k):**
- Contributions reduce taxable income NOW
- Money grows tax-deferred
- Pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement

**Roth 401(k):**
- Contribute after-tax dollars
- Money grows tax-free
- Withdraw tax-free in retirement

### 401(k) Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Higher contribution limits than IRA
- Employer matching (free money!)
- Automatic payroll deductions
- Creditor protection

**Cons:**
- Limited investment options
- Higher fees in some plans
- Must leave employer to rollover
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

## What Is an IRA?

An Individual Retirement Account (IRA) is a retirement account you open yourself at a brokerage. You're in complete control of contributions and investments.

### Types of IRAs

**Traditional IRA:**
- Tax-deductible contributions (income limits apply)
- Tax-deferred growth
- Taxed upon withdrawal

**Roth IRA:**
- No tax deduction on contributions
- Tax-free growth
- Tax-free withdrawals in retirement
- No RMDs during your lifetime

### Key Features of IRA

**Contribution Limits (2026):**
- Under 50: $7,000
- Age 50+: $8,000 (includes $1,000 catch-up)

**Income Limits for Roth IRA (2026):**
- Single: Phase-out $150,000-$165,000
- Married Filing Jointly: Phase-out $236,000-$246,000

**Investment Freedom:**
- Stocks, bonds, ETFs, mutual funds
- REITs, options, some alternative investments
- Choose any brokerage (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab)

### IRA Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Unlimited investment options
- Lower fees (you choose your investments)
- No employer required
- Roth IRA has no RMDs

**Cons:**
- Lower contribution limits
- No employer match
- Income limits for Roth contributions
- Requires self-discipline to contribute

## Traditional vs Roth: Which Tax Treatment?

### Choose Traditional If:
- You're in a high tax bracket now
- You expect lower income in retirement
- You need the tax deduction today
- You're close to retirement

### Choose Roth If:
- You're in a lower tax bracket now
- You expect higher income in retirement
- You're young with decades of growth ahead
- You want tax-free income in retirement

**The Math:**
If your tax rate stays the same, Traditional and Roth end up equal. The difference is WHEN you pay taxes, not IF.

## The Optimal Strategy: Use Both

Here's the recommended priority order:

### Step 1: 401(k) Up to Employer Match
- ALWAYS capture free money first
- Even if the 401(k) has mediocre options

### Step 2: Max Out Roth IRA
- Better investment options
- Tax-free growth
- No RMDs

### Step 3: Max Out 401(k)
- Return to 401(k) after IRA is maxed
- Higher limits allow more tax-advantaged saving

### Step 4: Backdoor Roth IRA (If High Income)
- For those exceeding Roth income limits
- Contribute to Traditional IRA, convert to Roth
- Consult a tax professional

### Step 5: Taxable Brokerage
- After maxing tax-advantaged accounts
- Still beneficial for long-term investing

## Special Situations

### Self-Employed? Consider:
- **Solo 401(k):** Up to $69,000 in 2026
- **SEP IRA:** Up to 25% of net self-employment income
- **SIMPLE IRA:** $16,500 employee contribution + employer match

### Changing Jobs?
- Roll old 401(k) into new employer's 401(k)
- Or roll into IRA for more investment options
- Never cash out (huge tax penalty!)

### Spouse Doesn't Work?
- Spousal IRA allows non-working spouse to contribute
- Based on working spouse's income
- Same $7,000 limit

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Not Getting Full Employer Match
Leaving match money on the table is literally refusing free money.

### 2. Cashing Out When Changing Jobs
You'll pay income tax PLUS 10% penalty. Roll it over instead.

### 3. Only Using One Account Type
Tax diversification matters. Have both Traditional and Roth money.

### 4. Being Too Conservative When Young
With decades until retirement, stocks outperform bonds long-term.

### 5. Ignoring Fees
High-fee funds can cost you hundreds of thousands over a career.

## Action Steps

1. **Check your 401(k):** What's the employer match? Contribute at least that much.

2. **Open an IRA:** Fidelity, Vanguard, or Schwab offer great options with no minimums.

3. **Decide Traditional vs Roth:** Based on your current and expected future tax rates.

4. **Automate contributions:** Set up automatic transfers so you never miss a month.

5. **Review annually:** Increase contributions when you get raises.

## The Bottom Line

**Best of both worlds:** Use your 401(k) for the employer match and higher contribution limits. Use your IRA for better investment options and flexibility.
