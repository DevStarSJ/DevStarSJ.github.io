Social Security is the foundation of retirement income for millions of Americans—and the decisions you make can mean a difference of **hundreds of thousands of dollars** over your lifetime. Here's how to maximize every dollar you've earned.

## How Social Security Benefits Are Calculated

Your benefit is based on:

1. **Your 35 highest-earning years** (adjusted for inflation)
2. **Your age when you claim** (between 62-70)
3. **Your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)** at full retirement age

The formula is progressive—lower earners replace a higher percentage of their income than higher earners.

## Strategy 1: Delay Benefits Until 70

This is the single most powerful strategy for most people.

| Claiming Age | Benefit vs. Full Retirement Age |
|--------------|--------------------------------|
| 62 | 25-30% less |
| Full Retirement (66-67) | 100% |
| 70 | 24-32% more |

**The math**: Benefits increase approximately 8% per year between full retirement age and 70.

**Example**: If your benefit at 67 is $2,000/month:
- At 62: ~$1,400/month
- At 70: ~$2,480/month

That's $13,000+ more per year—for life.

## Strategy 2: Work at Least 35 Years

Social Security averages your 35 highest-earning years. If you work fewer:

- **Zero-income years count as $0**
- Each zero year significantly lowers your average
- Working 35+ years replaces low-earning early years

**Action**: Even a few more years of work can meaningfully boost benefits.

## Strategy 3: Maximize Earnings in Peak Years

Your benefit calculation uses inflation-adjusted earnings, but there are caps:

- **2026 taxable maximum**: ~$176,100 (estimated)
- Earnings above this don't count toward benefits

**Strategy**: If you're close to peak earning years (usually 45-60), maximize income during this window. Consider:
- Salary negotiation
- Side income
- Delayed retirement if earning potential is high

## Strategy 4: Coordinate Spousal Benefits

Married couples have unique optimization opportunities:

### Spousal Benefit Basics
- One spouse can claim up to 50% of the other's PIA
- You must be 62+ and your spouse must have filed for benefits
- You receive the higher of your own benefit or spousal benefit

### Optimal Strategy
- Higher earner delays until 70 (maximizes lifetime household income)
- Lower earner may claim earlier to provide income while waiting
- Consider age gaps—if one spouse is much younger, timing matters more

### Survivor Benefits
When one spouse dies, the survivor receives the higher of:
- Their own benefit
- 100% of the deceased spouse's benefit

This makes delaying the higher earner's benefit even more valuable.

## Strategy 5: Understand the Earnings Test

If you claim before full retirement age and continue working:

- **2026 limit** (estimated): ~$23,400/year
- For every $2 over the limit, $1 is withheld from benefits
- In the year you reach FRA: $1 withheld for every $3 over a higher limit

**Good news**: Withheld benefits aren't lost—they're added back after FRA, increasing your monthly benefit.

**Strategy**: If you plan to work, consider waiting to claim until you'll earn under the limit or reach FRA.

## Strategy 6: Minimize Taxes on Benefits

Up to 85% of Social Security benefits can be taxable depending on your "combined income":

**Combined Income** = Adjusted Gross Income + Nontaxable Interest + 50% of SS Benefits

| Filing Status | Combined Income | Taxable Portion |
|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Single | <$25,000 | 0% |
| Single | $25,000-$34,000 | Up to 50% |
| Single | >$34,000 | Up to 85% |
| Married | <$32,000 | 0% |
| Married | $32,000-$44,000 | Up to 50% |
| Married | >$44,000 | Up to 85% |

### Tax Reduction Strategies
- Draw from Roth accounts (not counted as income)
- Manage IRA withdrawals strategically
- Consider Roth conversions before claiming SS
- Time large capital gains to avoid threshold jumps

## Strategy 7: Consider Your Health and Longevity

The "break-even" point between claiming early vs. late is typically around age 80-82.

**Claim earlier if:**
- Poor health or family history of shorter lifespan
- You need the income immediately
- You have no other income sources

**Delay if:**
- Good health and longevity in your family
- You have other assets to live on
- Your spouse will rely on survivor benefits

**Remember**: Social Security is longevity insurance. The longer you live, the more delaying pays off.

## Strategy 8: Avoid Common Costly Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Claiming at 62 "To Get Your Money"
- You've paid in, but starting early permanently reduces benefits
- Calculate your lifetime benefit, not just monthly

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Spousal Coordination
- Couples often claim independently without strategizing
- This can cost tens of thousands in lost benefits

### Mistake 3: Not Checking Your Earnings Record
- Errors happen—review your Social Security statement annually
- Corrections must be made within 3 years, 3 months of the error year

### Mistake 4: Forgetting About Medicare
- Medicare eligibility starts at 65, separate from Social Security
- You can have Medicare without claiming SS benefits

### Mistake 5: Assuming Benefits Won't Be There
- Social Security has funding challenges but isn't going bankrupt
- Even worst-case projections show 75-80% of benefits paid

## Final Thoughts

Social Security isn't something to figure out at 62. The decisions you make years before retirement—from your earnings to your claiming strategy—can add up to a six-figure difference in lifetime benefits.

Take the time to understand your options. For something you've paid into your entire career, it's worth getting right.
