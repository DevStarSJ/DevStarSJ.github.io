## What Is a Sinking Fund?

A sinking fund is money you set aside gradually for a planned future expense. Instead of scrambling for $1,200 when car insurance is due, you save $100/month for 12 months beforehand.

**The key difference from emergency funds:**
- Emergency fund = Unexpected expenses (job loss, medical emergency)
- Sinking fund = Expected expenses (holidays, vacations, car repairs)

## Why Sinking Funds Work

### 1. Eliminates Budget-Busting "Surprises"
That $600 vet bill isn't a surprise—pets need care. A sinking fund makes you ready.

### 2. Reduces Financial Stress
Knowing you have money set aside for Christmas shopping in July feels amazing.

### 3. Stops the Debt Cycle
Without sinking funds, unexpected bills often land on credit cards at 20%+ interest.

### 4. Makes Budgeting Realistic
A budget that ignores annual expenses isn't actually a budget—it's wishful thinking.

## How to Set Up Sinking Funds

### Step 1: List Your Planned Expenses

Grab last year's bank/credit card statements and find:
- Annual bills
- Seasonal spending
- Irregular but predictable expenses

### Step 2: Calculate Monthly Contributions

Formula: **Total cost ÷ Months until needed = Monthly savings**

| Expense | Annual Cost | Months | Monthly Savings |
|---------|-------------|--------|-----------------|
| Car Insurance | $1,200 | 12 | $100 |
| Holiday Gifts | $600 | 12 | $50 |
| Vacation | $2,400 | 12 | $200 |
| Car Repairs | $1,000 | 12 | $83 |
| **Total** | **$5,200** | | **$433** |

### Step 3: Choose Where to Keep Them

**Option 1: Separate Bank Accounts**
- Open high-yield savings accounts for each fund
- Pro: Crystal clear tracking
- Con: Multiple accounts to manage

**Option 2: One Account with Spreadsheet Tracking**
- Keep all sinking funds in one HYSA
- Track allocations in a spreadsheet
- Pro: Simpler banking
- Con: Requires discipline

**Option 3: Envelope System (Physical or Digital)**
- Allocate cash or use apps like YNAB
- Pro: Visual and tangible
- Con: Cash doesn't earn interest

### Step 4: Automate Contributions

Set up automatic transfers on payday:
- Checking → Individual sinking fund accounts
- Or: Checking → Master sinking fund account (track in spreadsheet)

## Sinking Funds vs. Emergency Fund: What Goes Where?

**Use Emergency Fund for:**
- Job loss
- Major medical emergency
- Essential home repairs (furnace dies in winter)
- True surprises you couldn't predict

**Use Sinking Funds for:**
- Predictable annual expenses
- Planned purchases
- Known upcoming costs
- Things on your calendar

**Rule of thumb**: If you know it's coming, it's a sinking fund expense.

## How Many Sinking Funds Is Too Many?

Start with 3-5 categories based on your biggest pain points. Too many funds = analysis paralysis.

**Starter Set:**
1. Car maintenance/repairs
2. Holiday spending
3. Annual subscriptions
4. Medical/dental
5. Fun money (vacation/entertainment)

**Advanced Set (add as needed):**
- Home repairs
- Pet fund
- Clothing
- Technology
- Gifts (birthdays, weddings)

## Tips for Sinking Fund Success

### 1. Name Your Funds Specifically
"Vacation: Hawaii 2026" is more motivating than "Travel Fund"

### 2. Review Monthly
Check balances, adjust contributions if goals change

### 3. Replenish After Spending
Once you use the car repair fund, start building it back up

### 4. Don't Borrow Between Funds
Christmas money shouldn't become vacation money

### 5. Round Up Contributions
$47 calculated? Save $50. The extra adds up.

### 6. Celebrate Hitting Goals
Funded your entire Christmas budget by October? That's worth celebrating!

## What If You Can't Afford All the Sinking Funds?

**Prioritize ruthlessly:**

1. **Must-have**: Annual bills that cause overdrafts (insurance, taxes)
2. **Should-have**: Predictable needs (car repairs, medical)
3. **Nice-to-have**: Wants (vacation, upgrades)

Start where you can. Even $25/month toward car repairs is better than $0.
